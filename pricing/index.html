02 · The price

One price. One promise.

Plumbline is $4.99 CAD a month, with a 7-day free trial at the front of it. There is no annual discount, no enterprise tier, no family plan, no API add-on, and no upsell ladder waiting a few weeks in. There is one product, one price, and one promise — that the subscription earns itself, every month, by being worth the cost of a sandwich a week.

A short note on why the price is what it is. $4.99 CAD a month is not a budget choice and it is not a competitive move. Plumbline is not trying to undercut Questrade, where you transact cheaply, because Plumbline is not a brokerage. Plumbline is not trying to be a free alternative to a paid terminal, because Plumbline is not a terminal. The price is a filter. It is the smallest reasonable amount that says: this is a serious subscription, for a serious practice, and you pay it because you read your own book — not because the product is cheap.

If you are already the kind of investor who has a brokerage — Questrade, Wealthsimple, IBKR Canada, one of the big five direct-investing platforms — reads the Globe and Mail's business section or the Financial Post, and is looking for a way to read your own positions with more discipline and less noise, the price is the smallest part of the test. The test is whether the read earns the subscription. The trial is the read, for seven days, on your real holdings.

03 · How Plumbline differs

The discipline of a terminal, the readability of a notebook.

Three distinctions, in prose rather than a feature matrix. The audience this product is built for already knows the alternatives; what follows is how Plumbline sits in relation to them.

Vs. a brokerage

Questrade, Wealthsimple, IBKR, the big-five

A Canadian discount brokerage — Questrade, Wealthsimple, IBKR Canada, the big-five direct-investing arms — is the place where you open an account, fund it, and place orders. The product is built around transactions. Plumbline is not a brokerage. Plumbline does not hold your money, does not hold your securities, does not custody your assets, and does not place orders on your behalf. The product is built around reading — around giving you a clear picture of the holdings you already have, in whatever account you already keep them, against the conditions around them. If you want to trade, your brokerage is the place. If you want to understand what you are holding, in a frame your brokerage does not give you, Plumbline is the place. The two are not in competition, because they are not doing the same job.

Vs. a professional terminal

The trading-floor species of product

A professional terminal — Bloomberg, FactSet, the kind of platform used on a trading floor and priced for the people whose job is finance — is a different species of product. Those tools reward fluency. They assume you already know what an order book is, what a duration number means, what a credit spread looks like when it widens. Plumbline is built on the discipline of a terminal — the rigour of a 12-factor model, the freshness of the data, the habit of reading conditions before acting — and the readability of a notebook. The score, the brief, the factor explanations, the band vocabulary: all of it is designed to give you the rigour of a terminal in a form you can actually read on the train. Plumbline is not a terminal you have to apprentice yourself to. It is a terminal's discipline, with the friction taken out.

Vs. a financial media site

Globe, Financial Post, BNN, the rest

A financial media site — the Globe and Mail's business section, the Financial Post, BNN Bloomberg, the Canadian arms of the global news firehoses — gives you news, but the news is not bound to your book. You can read every story the Financial Post publishes and still not know what the day meant for the eight positions you actually hold. Plumbline is bound to your holdings. The Daily Brief, the Pulse score, and the Markets Now tile are all built around the question what does today's news mean for the things I own, not around the question what happened in the news today. The difference is small in the abstract and large in practice.
