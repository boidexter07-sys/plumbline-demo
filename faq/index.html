$ · The 29-factor Pulse · explained

Six answers to the questions readers actually ask.

What changed in the robustness revision, what the 29 factors are, why five bands, how the cutpoints work, what the asset-class mask does, and why the price is $4.99.

01What changed in the robustness revision?

Five things, all Taha-confirmed 2026-06-24:

The math. The old normalization was signed — a good move raised the score, a bad move lowered it. Under that math, a holding in the middle of a crash would read Quiet. The new model reads magnitude: a move in either direction raises the score, by the same amount. The five band names survive. The 0–1 range survives. The bands finally mean what the copy says they mean.

The baselines. The old model used a 252-day window for every factor. A quarterly GDP print only appears four times in that window — there's not enough data to mean anything. Each factor now uses its own native-frequency history: quarterly over ten years, monthly over ten years, daily over 252 trading days.

The factor set. The 30-factor build is now a 29-factor build. F6 and F16 — the reciprocal CAD/USD and USD/CAD factors — are netted into a single exposure-scaled FX factor, weighted by each holding's USD revenue exposure. The freed weight is redistributed to F1 and F2 (the price lane).

The cutpoints. The numeric cutpoints between bands no longer live at 0.20 / 0.40 / 0.60 / 0.80. They are now derived from the empirical distribution of the composite across the universe and locked as percentiles. The names don't change. The default target shape is roughly: Quiet below 50th, Light 50th–75th, Active 75th–90th, Strong 90th–98th, Intense top 2%.

The asset-class mask. Crypto doesn't read Canadian-domestic macro. The mask is explicit: a token gets 20 factors, not 29. Equities get the full set. Commodities get most of it. The mask is honest about what the model is doing.

02What are the 29 factors?

Twenty-nine measurable inputs, organised in five lanes: price (4 factors), macro-FX (1 factor), macro-rest (17 factors), news (5 factors), sentiment (1 factor). The full table lives on the Model Details page. Some highlights, real term first, then the translation:

  • F1 · Return 1d — how the holding moved today vs. yesterday's close
  • F5 · BoC overnight rate — the Bank of Canada's policy rate and the direction it's moving
  • F9 · News tone shift — whether the tone of recent stories has shifted positive or negative, 7d window
  • F22 · 10y − 2y spread — the difference between US 10y and 2y yields; inverted for >1 quarter precedes recessions
  • F27 · Credit Spread (HY OAS) — extra yield junk-rated US borrowers pay over US Treasuries
  • F28 · VIX — what the options market is pricing for S&P 500 volatility over the next 30 days
03Why 5 bands, and what does each band mean?

Because conditions aren't a one-dimensional thing, and forcing them into “good” or “bad” throws away the nuance Pulse is meant to capture. The five bands describe how much is happening, not whether it is good or bad. A Quiet holding can be quietly in trouble. An Intense holding can be Intense for reasons you already understand and have a thesis on.

Quiet (below ~50th pct) — the holding is doing what it usually does. Light (50th–75th) — something small has shifted. Active (75th–90th) — conditions are in motion. Strong (90th–98th) — several signals are lined up. Intense (top ~2%) — most or all factors are signalling. Rare by construction.

04Why did the cutpoints change from 0.20 / 0.40 / 0.60 / 0.80 to percentiles?

The old 0.20-step cutpoints assumed an evenly distributed composite. Under the new intensity math, the composite's resting distribution shifts upward — a typical day for a typical holding is now around 0.45, not 0.50. Fixed cutpoints at 0.20 / 0.40 / 0.60 / 0.80 would make “Intense is rare” stop being true. Locking the cutpoints to the empirical percentiles preserves the rarity the band names promise.

The exact percentiles are Taha's call once the v1.0 backtest computes them. The five band names don't change. The 0–1 range doesn't change.

05What does the asset-class mask do?

It stops the model from applying factors that don't transmit to the holding. Canadian-domestic macro factors (F13 CPI, F14 gas, F18 renewables, F19 auto sales, F20 GDP per capita, F23 labour, F24 shelter, F25 trade balance) don't carry a signal to a Bitcoin holding. Under the old model, the macro lane would have inflated crypto's reading with factors that don't apply. The mask turns those factors off for crypto. Equities get the full 29. Commodities get 27 (F1 and F2 dialed down because there's no sector-peers line for crude).

The factors that do apply carry more weight within the smaller set. The reading is more honest for a token, not less.

06Why is the price $4.99 CAD per month?

Because the data costs us $0/month. Every source Pulse reads is free and public — Yahoo Finance, Statistics Canada, the Federal Reserve's FRED, GDELT, Polymarket, Kalshi, PredictIt, the BoC Valet API. The only net-new infrastructure cost in v1.0 is the ALFRED archival feed (also free, FRED-adjacent) for the v1.1 backtest. There is no margin pressure on the data side, so the price is what it is.
