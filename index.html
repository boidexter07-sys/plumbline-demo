02 · The way it works

Three layers, read in order.

Plumbline is built around a sequence — first the seeing, then the understanding, then the calm decision that comes after, made by you, in your own time. The three layers are treated on their own terms, and each is worth a few minutes of explanation.

i.
See

See your investments clearly.

The first thing Plumbline owes you is a clear picture of what you own. Not a wall of tickers. Not a sea of red and green. A picture. Each holding appears once, with the things that actually matter about it in plain sight — what it is, what sector it lives in, how it has moved over a window long enough to be meaningful, and the Pulse score that says, in one number, how much attention the position is asking for right now. The price is there, but it is not the loudest thing on the page. The position is the unit, not the price chart.

If you already have multiple platforms — and most active Canadian investors do — the seeing is the part that has quietly gotten worse, not better, as more tools have arrived. The brokerage shows you positions the way the brokerage wants to show them, optimised for the order ticket. The Wealthsimple-style app shows you positions optimised for engagement, which usually means the loudest movers. The crypto exchange shows you the tokens, in isolation, without any sense of how the position sits next to the rest of the book. The RRSP lives at one institution. The TFSA lives at another. The non-registered cash account lives somewhere else entirely. None of these surfaces are wrong for what they are. They are just not built to answer the question that matters most for a real reader: what do I actually own, and how is it doing, in one frame, right now?

Plumbline's read is different because it shows you the underlying signals behind the prices, not just the prices. A concrete example. You hold Shopify (SHOP on TSX). On the brokerage screen, the line is up 2% on the day, which tells you nothing. On Plumbline, the same line sits next to a Pulse that says, in one sentence, that the move aligns with a geopolitical signal (a relevant event surfaced through the GDELT news layer), a positive news tone shift over the last week, and a technical signal (the price action against the stock's own recent history) that agrees. Or, on a different day, the same holding shows up with the same 2% move but a Pulse that says the price is moving while the signals around it are not — the technical signal is up, but the news tone has cooled and the macro signal is flat. The price is the same. The read is not. That difference is what Layer 1 is for.

03 · How Pulse is built

What Pulse is, and why it exists.

Every holding in your portfolio carries a number called Pulse. The number runs from 0 to 100, and it sits in one of five named bands: Quiet, Light, Active, Strong, or Intense. The number changes through the week as the conditions around the holding change. It is not a price. It is not a forecast. It is a single, blended read on how much attention a position deserves from you today, given what is happening in the market and in the world around the market.

What Pulse measures

Pulse is built from a 12-factor model. A factor in this context is one measurable input — a number about price behaviour, macro conditions, or news tone — that Pulse takes into account when computing the score. Twelve factors, each one doing a different job. Some come from the market itself: price movement over different windows, how a holding has behaved relative to its own history, how it has behaved relative to the sector it lives in, how its volatility (the size of its day-to-day swings) has changed over the recent past. Some come from the wider world: Bank of Canada overnight rate conditions, currency moves, commodity pressures, the kind of macro weather that quietly shapes every position in your book whether you are watching it or not. And some come from the news — drawn from a curated set of Canadian and global financial sources, indexed through GDELT (the Global Database of Events, Language, and Tone, a real-time index of world events and the language surrounding them) — and weighted for tone and direction rather than raw volume.

No single factor drives the score. The twelve are blended together, weighted by how much each tends to matter in the current environment, and the result is the number on the screen. When you tap on a Pulse score, the factors that pushed it up or pulled it down are listed for you. Nothing is hidden. Nothing is dressed up. You can see the ingredients. You can disagree with the outcome. And if you disagree, you have the vocabulary to explain why, which is the part of investing nobody teaches you and the part that compounds over years.

A note on the asset universe: Pulse runs on the full S&P 500, the top ten cryptocurrencies by market capitalisation, and ten major commodities — gold, silver, oil, natural gas, wheat, corn, soybeans, copper, platinum, and lithium. Canadian-listed names (TSX and TSXV) are in scope, and most major U.S. names are interlisted on TSX in CAD, which means Plumbline shows you the price on the exchange you actually hold the position on, with both legs visible. You do not need to think about where a ticker lives to read its Pulse.

What the five bands mean

A Quiet Pulse means the holding is doing roughly what it usually does. Nothing is asking for your attention. A Light Pulse means something small has shifted — not enough to act on, but worth being aware of. An Active Pulse means conditions around the holding are in motion, in one direction or another, and a thoughtful look is reasonable. A Strong Pulse means several factors are moving at once, and the position deserves a careful read. An Intense Pulse means the holding is in the middle of something significant — a regime change (a meaningful shift in the prevailing market environment), a sharp move, a meaningful news shift — and ignoring it would be a choice, not a default.

The bands are observational, not directional. Strong does not mean good. Quiet does not mean safe. A Quiet holding in a sector under pressure is only a holding the score has not yet caught up to. An Intense holding in a sector you understand and have a long thesis on (a written reason for owning it that you can still defend) is not a signal to panic. The bands describe the conditions. What those conditions mean for you, given your own book and your own horizon, is your call. Pulse gives you the read. It does not pretend to know what you should do with it.

What Pulse does not do

Pulse does not predict. It does not forecast. It does not tell you what a stock will do tomorrow, next week, or next year. It is a description of present conditions, not a guess about future ones. Pulse does not tell you to buy, to sell, to trim, to add, or to hold. There is no action language anywhere in the score, the bands, or the factors. Pulse does not rank holdings against each other in a way that implies one is better than another. Two positions can both be Active for entirely different reasons, and the score will not flatten that into a leaderboard.

The reason for these refusals is the reason the product exists at all. Plumbline is for observation. The score earns its value by being trustworthy, and a score that whispers buy or sell stops being a score — it becomes a recommendation dressed up as a number. Pulse is a number. What you do with it is yours.

06 · Questions

Things people ask before signing up.

Plain answers to the questions readers ask before they hand over their email and a credit card.

01What is Pulse, in real terms?

Pulse is a number from 0 to 100 that sits on every holding in your portfolio, with a band name attached. The bands are Quiet, Light, Active, Strong, and Intense. The number describes how much attention the position is asking for from you today, given the market conditions around it and the world around the market. It is built from twelve different inputs — some about price behaviour, some about the macro economy, some about the news — that are blended into a single read. The number changes through the week as the conditions change. It is a description of what is happening now, not a forecast of what will happen next.

02How is Pulse different from a Morningstar rating or an analyst recommendation?

An analyst rating — Buy / Hold / Sell, or the star system at Morningstar — is an opinion about whether a stock is worth owning, expressed as a long-term call. Pulse is none of those things. Pulse does not tell you whether a stock is a buy. Pulse tells you whether the conditions around a stock are asking for your attention today. A position can sit at Strong Pulse for a week because of macro turbulence, and the Morningstar rating can remain four stars, and both can be true at the same time — they are answering different questions. The rating answers is this a good long-term position. Pulse answers is something happening around this position right now that I should read carefully before I act. Different jobs. Different surfaces.

03Why a 12-factor model? What are the 12 factors?

Because no single number describes a position's conditions reliably on its own. A stock can be moving on price while the news is flat and the macro is pushing the other way — the price factor says one thing, the news tone factor says another, and the truth is somewhere in the blend. The twelve factors split the work: some are price-derived (returns over different windows, volatility, relative strength against the sector, drawdown depth), some are macro (Bank of Canada overnight rate, currency moves, commodity pressure, sector rotation), and some are news-derived (tone shift over a rolling window, event volume, story direction, sourced through GDELT's index of global events). No factor drives the score alone. The factors are documented in full inside the product — when you tap a Pulse score, you see which factors are pushing up and which are pulling down, in plain terms.

04Why 5 bands, and what does each band mean?

Because conditions are not a one-dimensional thing, and forcing them into "good" or "bad" throws away the nuance the score is meant to capture. Quiet (0–24) means the holding is doing what it usually does and nothing is asking for your attention. Light (25–49) means something small has shifted, worth being aware of but not enough to act on. Active (50–74) means conditions around the holding are in motion and a thoughtful look is reasonable. Strong (75–89) means several factors are moving and a careful read is warranted. Intense (90–100) means the holding is in the middle of something significant, and ignoring it would be a choice, not a default. The bands describe how much is happening, not whether it is good or bad. A Quiet holding can be quietly in trouble. An Intense holding can be Intense for reasons you already understand and have a thesis on. The bands give you the conditions. The judgment is yours.

05What does a 'Watch' band mean — should I act on it?

A Watch band (Light, Active, Strong, or Intense) means conditions around the position are asking for some level of attention, in increasing degrees of seriousness. None of them are a signal to do anything. The bands do not have opinions on whether you should buy, sell, trim, or hold. They have opinions on whether the conditions around the position are worth noticing. Reading carefully, in your own time, is the right response. Acting on a band reading — without doing the read — is exactly the kind of hurried decision the product is built to discourage. Plumbline cannot be held liable for any investment outcome resulting from information surfaced by the service.

06Does Pulse predict prices?

No. Pulse is a description of present conditions, not a forecast of future ones. The twelve factors are blended using recent history, weighted by how much each tends to matter in the current environment, and the result is a number that reflects what is happening now. The score changes through the week as the inputs change. The score has no opinion about where the price will be next week or next year. Treating Pulse as a forecast would be a category error — Pulse is the read, not the prediction. If you want a forecast, no product can give you a reliable one, and any product that claims to is selling something else.

07How fresh is the data?

The underlying factors refresh on different cadences, depending on what they measure. Price-related factors refresh through each trading session. Macro factors refresh on the schedule of their data sources — daily for some indicators, intraday for others. News factors refresh as new stories surface, weighted for tone and direction over a rolling window. The Pulse number on a holding may move several times in a given week, and may sit still for a day or two at a stretch. There is no benefit to watching it tick by tick. The score is meant to be read once a day, not once a minute. The product is built to slow you down, not to make you faster.

08Will Plumbline work with my RRSP, TFSA, or non-registered account?

Yes. Plumbline is a read-only analytical surface, and it does not care which kind of account your positions live in. You enter the tickers and position sizes yourself — by hand, by spreadsheet import, or through the brokerage file-format imports we support at launch — and the product shows you a Pulse score on every position regardless of whether it sits inside an RRSP, a TFSA, an FHSA, or a non-registered margin or cash account. Plumbline does not connect to your brokerage, does not request your brokerage credentials, and does not have read access to any of your accounts at any institution. The portfolio you see is the portfolio you have entered. This is by design, and the choice is deliberate: Plumbline is the read, not the order ticket. Your accounts stay where they are. You stay in control.

Your portfolio, Pulse history, sandbox positions, and Daily Brief archive remain exportable for ninety days after cancellation, in standard CSV or JSON formats, and are then permanently deleted. You can export at any point during the ninety-day window from the settings page. You can also delete the account outright, which removes everything within a short window and cannot be undone. There is no retention call, no exit survey, no friction. The cancellation path is the proof of the product's confidence in the subscription.
